ABOUT

“Study nature, love nature, stay close to nature. It will never fail you.”

Frank Lloyd Wright was an American architect, designer, writer, and educator. He designed more than 1,000 structures over a creative period of 70 years. Wright played a key role in the architectural movements of the twentieth century, influencing architects worldwide through his works and hundreds of apprentices in his Taliesin Fellowship.
He started out as a modern architect, but moved on to contemporary architecture at the end of his life. Not only did he fuse Eastern and Western architecture, but he also helped American architecture, which had been a copy of European architecture until then, develop its own style and show the way forward to modern architecture.
He broke away from European housing styles and pursued a unique American housing style, the “Prairie Style.” What he followed in his architectural philosophy was the "Organic" concept, and he thought that architecture was about designing a space that naturally blends into its surroundings rather than controlling nature. This attitude of placing importance on nature harmonizes with the natural environment of vast grasslands unique to the United States, creating the "Prairie House Style". The form is strongly horizontal. This matches the endless horizon of the American plains.
Having studied the encounter between nature and architecture, he attempts this encounter even in the city. In the Larkin Building, built in Buffalo, New York, in 1904, the office building opens inward rather than outward, and has windows in the ceiling to allow access to outside air and natural light. This building is one of the first office buildings to be equipped with environmental controls.
Frank Lloyd Wright's buildings were listed as World Cultural Heritage at the 43rd World Heritage Committee meeting held in Baku, Azerbaijan in 2019.
