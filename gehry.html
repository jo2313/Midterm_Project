ABOUT

"Architecture should speak of its time and place, but yearn for timelessness."

Frank Gehry is a Canadian-born American architect and designer. He spent time drawing with his father, and his mother introduced him to the world of art. He won the Pritzker Prize, often referred to as the Nobel Prize of architecture, in 1989. Gehry is a master architect who represented Deconstructionist architecture in the 2000s. He is an architect who most clearly expressed the sculptural form of architecture through the free flow of curved surfaces that appear to flutter in the wind and the high-tech texture of titanium.
He moved to Los Angeles at the age of 16, and the free-spirited, open, and diverse atmosphere of the city is said to have greatly influenced his work.
After graduating from school, he worked at Victor Gruen Associates, where he interacted with many artists. It is said that this experience served as an opportunity for him to become an "Architect Like An Artist."
Gehry's use of corrugated steel, chain-link fencing, unpainted plywood, and other utilitarian or "Everyday" materials was partly inspired by spending Saturday mornings at his grandfather's hardware store.
Unlike his later masterpieces, during his early days, he actively experimented with the physical properties of materials, using inexpensive materials like wire mesh, corrugated steel plates, and recycled wood. However, his avant-garde architectural work was marginalized as non-mainstream at the time. His project to renovate his own house in 1978 was harshly criticized by the architectural community as "Garbage Architecture."
In the 1990s, Frank Gehry established his own technology research center and introduced the "CATIA" system, which was originally designed for shaping French aircraft. This was because existing companies were not perfect at twisting and bending metal panels as he wanted, and the prices were prohibitively high.

SKETCHES

If you look at Frank Gehry's sketches, they may evoke a sense of Graffiti. In Frank Gehry's work process, the "Ambiguity" of his sketches is considered a unique source of power exclusive to his work. This is because his abstract and energetic sketches serve as a continual source of inspiration for both the building itself and his colleagues, including clients.
